Fourth Amendment

The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.

Analysis

The Fourth Amendment is the protection against unreasonable searches. It requires that warrants be issued only with probable cause. This is crucial for protecting individuals from government overreach. It ensures personal space, both in the physical and digital world. For example, this is seen in modern ruling on cell phone searches. This amendment ensures a balance between public safety and individual liberty.

Eighth Amendment

Excessive bail shall not be required, nor excessive fines imposed, nor cruel and unusual punishments inflicted.

Analysis

The Eighth Amendment ensures that a person's freedom before trial is not determined by their ability to pay. The first clause, “excessive bail shall not be required,” protects people who have been accused but not yet convicted of a crime. The second clause, “nor cruel and unusual punishments inflicted,” refers to the debate over the death penalty or other forms of punishment. This amendment continuously checks on the power of the state to punish citizens to ensure that the punishment fits the crime.
