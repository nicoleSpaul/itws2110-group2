History

Before any constitution was drafted, the American colonies were governed under charters by Britain. They became increasingly resentful of British control and declared themselves free states in 1776. They created the Articles of Confederation as the US’s first governing framework, which was in effect from 1781 to 1789. It established a weak central government with most power given to the 13 sovereign states. However, some limitations, like the inability to tax or enforce laws, led to financial instability and state disputes. A stronger US Constitution was ultimately needed to replace it. Delegates from 12 of the 13 states met in Philadelphia from May - September 1787 to revise the Articles of Confederation; this is known as the Constitutional Convention, which quickly escalated to a full rewrite.

Key issues were debated at the convention: how states would be represented in the new government, the role of slavery, the power of the executive branch, and the balance between state and federal authority. Larger states wanted to be represented by their population, whereas smaller states wanted to be represented equally; this led to the House of Representatives being based on state population and the Senate granting equal representation to each state. Slavery was addressed through the Three-Fifths compromise, stating that enslaved individuals counted as three-fifths of a person and that the slave trade could not be banned until 1808. The president was also designed at the convention and was granted this position significant powers that were subject to checks and balances through Congress and the judiciary. The final draft was signed on September 17, 1787. The Federalists, who supported the ratification of the Constitution, won support through the promise of a Bill of Rights, which was adopted in 1791 as the first ten amendments.

Today, the Constitution is considered a “living document” and continues to change based on amendments, court rulings, and political debate. The separation of powers and division of authority between states and the national government has allowed the Constitution to be the longest-lasting written constitution in the world.
